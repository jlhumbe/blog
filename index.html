August 23, 2018: The Work Cup

The quick, easy and decent cup of coffee. It pair's especially well with being a poor graduate student and office building funk.

It's 1:37 PM on a Wednesday. You thought that tuperware full of leftovers from last night's dinner would give you the fuel you need to power through the rest of the work day, but, as always, all they prepared you for was a nap. What do you do? Coffee, of course.

Maybe you are lucky and they provide coffee at your office, but unless you're in one of those snobby new age offices where they act like they 'respect' their employees and have a fresh espresso bar, chances are the tar like liquid that's been sitting in the office coffee pot since 7AM isn't the pick-me-up you're looking for. So, if you're anything like me, you take things into your own hands.

Sure, you could buy a small drip coffee machine and one of those colossal containers of Foldgers, but what's the fun in that? I mean, we are Millennials and that means we have an obligation to take easy tasks and make them enormously more complicated to obtain marginally better results. This leads me to my methods for, The Work Cup. Here's what you'll need:

A) The Gear

For starters, we're not looking to make the world's best cup of coffee (I mean, who can compete with these people). What we are looking to do is make a decent tasting cup with minimal financial and time costs. We'd also like a setup we can easily store at our desk. Enter the AeroPress. The AeroPress entered the markets in 2005 and has become famous for its simplicity and effectiveness in making a single cup of coffee. Grounds go in, water goes in, coffee squirts out directly into a cup. The unique strength of the AeroPress actually comes into play during the squirting part (NO! Stop it! She did not say that!). Pushing the plunger through the chamber creates pressure that forces the coffee infused water through a paper filter, and near the end of the processes, expels air through the grounds which helps to create a crema like top akin to what you might see from a real espresso machine.

This setup alone is great. It certainly does the trick. But alas, it was just too simple. To help us complicate things, the folks over at Fellow made an additional attachment called the prismo. The prismo is a nipple like contraption that attaches to the end of the AeroPress where the included filter holder used to go.

As I understand it, the prismo uses a very fine metal filter and a simple pressure valve to force pressure to build up within the AeroPress chamber before coffee (or air) shoot out like a squirt gun. The idea is that this helps to further mimic an espresso machine and provide that crema topping that really makes an espresso special. BUT, if you're a real rebel, you may have considered an additional perk to the prismo: it can be used even when making a full cup of coffee!

Now that you've picked up the pieces of your mind that just blew up all over when I told you that, let's look at the process...

B) Methods

1. Prepare your aeropress with the plunger just inserted into the non-hexagonal end of the Aeropress. Place a paper filter over top of the metal mesh filter in the prismo.

2. Acquire hot water. I get my hot water from this magic spout (as indicated by the red lever) which produces near boiling water on demand. I recommend using an insulated cup of some sort so you don't burn your hand off and your water stays hot. While not vacuum insulated, this sweet vessel has a layer of old school air insulation that does the trick.

3. Pour a small amount of the hot water into the AeroPress Chamber, swish it around a bit to warm up the plastic, and then pour it out.

4. Take a large heaping scoop of your finely ground coffee beans. The pre-ground espresso like "Cafe Bustello", "Medaglia D'oro" or "Pico" are great in that they are well ground and extremely cheap. Their taste is reasonably good (though simple). I have my "fancy" coffee in the morning and these in the afternoon, but I'm sure that process could be quite easily adapted to some specialty single-origin beans grown in the shade of an ancient olive tree on the side of Mt. Kilimanjaro... or you know, something like that. I think the real key is for the beans to be quite finely ground though. Place the scoop of ground beans into the bottom of the AeroPress chamber (against the top of the plunger).

5. Add hot water to the chamber, filling it about half way.

6. Use your stirring device to mix the grounds and hot water.

7. Continue to add water to the chamber, filling it nearly but not quite to the top.

8. Affix you prismo with the metal and paper filters and let it sit for up to 30 seconds. During this time, pour some hot water into your mug, swish it around and pour it out to pre-heat the ceramic.

9. Turn over the AeroPress to place the prismo into the mug. Apply pressure to the plunger to squirt coffee into the mug. Near the end of the extraction processes, the resistance should subside as the plunger expels the small bit of air left in the chambers to produce a nice crema like finish.

Clean up: Remove the prismo. Pull the paper filter out, toss (or compost) it and rinse the prismo. Directed into a trash can (or compost bin), continue to push on the plunger to expel grounds which should be squeezed into a puck like shape. Rinse anything else that needs and, tada, that's it.

If all goes well, you can be back in your swivel chair sipping at an incredibly okay cup of coffee in less than 3 minutes. MmmmMMmm, cheap and okay afternoon coffee -- the perfect Work Cup.

July 25, 2018: Healthy Bewilderment

Why it can be important to "know" less

You may have noticed a really quite incredible pattern amongst most people. It seems a vast majority of people possess an absolutely astonishing large breadth and depth of knowledge about, well, everything. I mean, so many of our politicians happen to also be leading experts in climate change, our wealthy businessmen impressively know what's best for the poor and, despite having never studied the body since high-school biology, just about everyone knows the best possible extreme diet to remain healthy and stop those horrible oxidants from oxidizing our, ugh, umm, bodies?. Of course the obvious truth is we all actually know quite little about most things. Few understand many of the detailed inner workings of our bodies or our planet and absolutely no one all of them. Some know a lot about a little, others a little of a lot, but no one really knows a lot about a lot.

So then why do we all, who know so little, pretend to know so much? Perhaps we can blame the internet, readily distributing both fact and fiction in record time, leading us to believe we know more than we do. Or, perhaps we can point the finger at science, constantly publishing seemingly contrary studies and thus supposedly spreading misinformation. I suppose it is also possible that we can just blame ourselves for accepting fictions as fact and point observations as fundamental natural laws (but this is probably not the case because we actually know a lot and couldn't make such mistakes, right?). And it certainly can't be the case that we purposefully misrepresent our knowledge in attempts to gain respect or promotion, can it?

I've been considering this question after starting a book recommended by my brother: Behave: the biology of humans at our best and worst by Robert M. Sapolsky. Early on in the book, he references Appendix A for a quick introduction to neurobiology, a daring task on his end. I am personally not a big fan of appendices because they feel disruptive and it feels kind of immoral to skip to the end and read a few pages, but my knowledge on neurosciences is beyond lacking, so I figured I would give it a look. The section starts by introducing the structure of a neuron, the fundamental building block of our nervous system and, consequently, the mechanisms through which we interpret and think about the world around us. His tactic for introducing the material is somewhat unusual but, in my mind, worthwhile. Piecewise, he describes how neurons work, only to start each subsequent sub-section with something like, "but, actually, not really."

See, despite being quite small (generally, but not always I learned), the workings of neurons and the nervous system they compose is vastly complex. Instead of diving immediately into that complexity, Sapolsky starts out simple, at a level I was actually familiar with: neurons send signals. I had long accepted this interpretation of neurons and while it isn't exactly wrong, it also isn't exactly correct. Neurons are not like mailmen, picking up signals in one place and dropping them off in another. As I read on, I learned about the complex behavior through which neurons 'hear' a signal through their dendrites, pass it along as either a resting or action potential by altering their polarity, and then, through their axonal endings, pass neurotransmitter on to the next neuron. Even this understanding is of course still juvenile. Understanding the full story has and continues to be the work of many careers.

And so, my thought was, can I claim to know how neurons work? My thought, particularly after reading this section, was no, I do not know how neurons work. I do not know what elements move around in which ways or how a complex web of trillions of neurons are able to work as a whole such that our brains can interpret their signals, and yet, I expect many who know even less than I of the workings of neurons may claim to know how they work. I do not mean to point the finger either, for in truth, when I knew even less about these processes, only that neurons carried signals, I too may have claimed such rights. I feel similarly about the issue of global climate change. On both sides of the isle, people who know little except maybe that carbon dioxide has something to do with it claim, "its really quite simple... ," but the thing is, it isn't! The factors contributing to global climate change are innumerable and vastly complex, and it is a grave but frequently made mistake to think otherwise. The point is, it is often a thick and hazy line to cross from 'not knowing' to 'knowing' and it can ironically take crossing over closer to the 'knowing' to be aware of the fact that we still don't know everything about even a single topic.

This is likely why so many scientist give really frustrating answers to questions. For instance, if asked if a volcano is going to erupt soon, a volcanologist may give an answer like, "There is a 55% chance the volcano will have a partial to full eruption in the next 50 to 2000 years." Talk about a non-answer! What the scientist is not saying but likely trying to imply is, "I've studied this system, it is brutally complex, and while I still don't fully know the answer to that question, I do have some guiding statistics that may be useful." He is further towards the "knowing" side than most on the subject, and from his perspective, he is aware of the vast complexities of the system which lead to uncertainty, thus the statistical answer rather than yes or no. But, if there is always this continuum from not knowing to knowing, the question then becomes, what, if anything, should we claim to know? Or, put another way, what should we admit to not knowing?

I think, but do not know, we should be more accepting of our own lack of knowledge. Maybe I feel this way only because I find myself saying, "I'm not sure" or "I don't know" all the time, but I also find myself carefully listening to information provided by others only to find later on that they actually didn't know what the hell they were talking about and I would have saved myself some time if I just listened to a rock instead. Not knowing something is not exactly useful, but it is also not damaging like pretending to know something can be. I believe this to be true not only in science, but in other parts of life. Do you know the directions to the beach? "No, I do not know the directions to the beach" is more useful than, "you're going to want to head west, then take maybe like 14 lefts, and then probably a right by some sort of building thing and then...(no idea where the beach is)." We as people have been working hard to figure out the world around us for many generations now and surely will continue to do so for many more. It is in our nature. We do this best though when we swallow our pride, admit what we don't know, and then work together to figure it out. Therefore, I say, be bewildered, accept confusion and admit to it freely. We all know very little in the scheme of things but we can get closer to a state of knowing, and by an easier route, if we accept our knowledge limitations and then address them.

June 21, 2018: Earth doesn't have a personality

But it may seem like it and we need to be weary of what will happen when it 'loses its cool'

Earth is generally forgiving to those who seek to change it. This, despite what we may want to believe, is not because it has a personality and cares about the well beings of the creatures inhabiting it. Instead, Earth tolerates change reasonably well because it hosts a complex web of life and physical systems which exhibit feedback mechanisms on each other. For example, if the population of one species suddenly grows rapidly, that species is likely to run out of food and eventually return to more typical levels. If ocean water is warmed in one area, currents will eventually move that water to cooler areas rather than allow it to continually heat up. If increased levels of carbon dioxide are released into the air, perhaps by volcanic activity or perhaps by cars' exhausts' pipes, the carbon dioxide is reasonably well dissipated throughout the atmosphere and absorbed by ocean waters. Plants and forests may also grow, taking up carbon dioxide, storing it and releasing oxygen.

These feedback systems are not infallible though, and when they break, it can be dramatic. When a large asteroid impacted the earth some 66 million years ago, it was such a dramatic even that it temporarily broke many of the feedback mechanisms keeping the world stable. A dense covering of debris and ash is believed to have blanketed the atmosphere, limiting sunlight, stopping much plant growth and driving the earth into severe winter like conditions for many years. Earth eventually recovered, obviously, but not until nearly 75% of species on earth went extinct.

While human activity is not impacting the Earth in such a dramatic way as did this asteroid 66 million years ago, it is disrupting many of the natural feedback mechanisms earth uses to maintain stable temperatures and other physical balances. The massive industrial excavation of carbon from underground in the form of coal, oil and gas, and the consequent releasing of it as carbon dioxide once it is burned, has started to take its toll on Earth's systems. As expected, the carbon dioxide has dispersed throughout the atmosphere, but more and more is introduced daily. The ocean has helped to buffer this increase by absorbing the gaseous carbon. As level increase though, this has also had the effect of making the oceans more acidic as the carbon dioxide dissociates into carbonic acid. The ocean too has its own buffers for such changes which have helped to slow this processes. Microscopic phytoplankton use the carbon dioxide in photosynthesis and many animals use the carbon in the water to create their shells. Still, as levels continue to rise, natural feedback mechanisms are unable to keep up. Ocean acidity is now responsible for bleaching coral reefs and accelerating the dissolution of some animals' shells.

There are many signs beginning to emerge that we are stretching Earth about as far as it is willing to go before it simply snaps. By conducting activities like the burning of fossil fuels and cutting down much of Earth's natural vegetation at the same time, we are doing the equivalent of pushing someone while another person sits crouched behind their legs. By disrupting the Earth's processes on multiple fronts over long periods of time, we are simply not allowing the Earth's natural buffers enough time to re-stabilize. One such sign is the longterm warming of the atmosphere and oceans. This means that carbon dioxide, which allows heat from the sun to reach the earth but can prevent heat from reflecting back away from the earth, is knocking Earth's heat balance out of whack. While it is true the Earth goes through natural temperature cycles, which are largely in response to predictable astronomical cycles and responsible for glacial and interglacial periods, there is, to say the least, an uncanny correlation between human activities and changes in Earth's behavior.

Because the Earth is so large and there are so many feedback systems buffering major changes, it has been slow to raise its fist, but it is important to understand that once certain systems break apart, they can take millions of years to put back together. One such system is that of ice. Over millions of years, water has evaporated from the oceans, formed clouds and been returned through precipitation. Some of that precipitation falls as snow which can accumulate for many many years on land. In addition to storing surprisingly large amounts of water on land, these large areas covered by snow and ice act to reflect the sun, much better than exposed land does. As we continue to release carbon dioxide, this ice will continue to melt which in turn causes more of the sun's energy to be absorbed, further warming the earth. This is an example of a positive feedback loop which, rather than slow change and maintain an equilibrium, can causes a run away effect where change accelerates over time. Just one reason we care about this is because a large majority of all people and trillions of dollars of infrastructure are located within a short distance of the coast. Melting land ice and a warming climate means sea levels will rise and large storms are likely to become more common and severe, further ravaging vulnerable coastal communities.

We are picking on something that we live on, and that is a dangerous thing to do. The Earth may not be conscious in the way we are, but we can already see it responding to our constant attacks. And, like it can be hard to tell exactly when or what pulls apart a person's last nerve before they snap, so it will be with the Earth, and the Earth's retaliation will be on a level commensurate with its size. So, the question is, are we really willing to risk testing the limits of the Earth before it snaps?

June 20, 2018: The Trifecta (aka the beach)

Just a few reasons why I'm weirdly obsessed with and study the coasts

I love the beach. And coasts in general. One of the neatest things about the coasts is their dynamicity. If you go to a beach one day and then a year later, it's unlikely it looks exactly the same. It may not even look the same from one day to the next if a strong storm hits. And this dynamic behavior is not limited to the visible section of the coast either. The shape of the bottom underneath the water can also also change continuously, something most surfers can attest to. A perfect sandbar won't be there forever

And it is not just the land that changes; the ocean, this astonishingly massive pool of salty water, is most times covered by a complex array of ripples which, to us, can appear as powerful, magnificent and even destructive waves. And where do the waves come from but the final piece of the trifecta; the air moving over the liquid creates these waves which may then travel for hundreds or even thousands of miles before crashing into a coast.

The coasts are the only places on earth where these three states of matter interact on such an immense scales. And while coasts may therefore receive the full furry of nature, they may also be one of the single most peaceful places to be. Almost everyone has walked up to a coastline at some point and just said, 'wow,' as most of their worries melt away. They are a place where, when nature turns her gentler side, we flock to for the sole purpose of being. Nowhere else do people come together and just sit in the glory of nature like they do on the coasts.

June 18, 2018: Usurping Evolution

Notes on the implications of understanding ourselves

It is an interesting point in time – at least for humans. We, collectively at a species, are the only known animal to have evolved in such a way that we have come to realize what biological evolution is, how it works and that it is the reason for our being here. It may seem an inconsequential and even philosophical consideration resulting from scientific inquiry, but to know how we came to be what we are may have some interesting implications. The strangeness of this knowledge, and what makes this such an interesting point in time for people, is that we are not limited to simply being aware of how evolution works. We can inspect it and asses it. Where evolution is limited to a single mind track – survive – we, though the processes of evolution, have come to a new era of awareness and prosperity.

Of course, a percentage of humans on this planet are not prospering. To many, the daily struggle for survival is all too real, and this is a genuine issue deserving of attention. As a whole though, our species is thriving. We have taken over the globe in such a way that no other animal poses any sort of threat to our species. We manipulate the earth to do as we will. We flattened it, plow it, and grow all the food we want, like no other animal ever has. We drain ancient pools of energy from deep underground to power machines that accelerate nearly all parts of our lives. We are gods, we think.

But, also, we at times digress. For instance, if we have come to pick apart the very history that created us, mistake by mistake, why can we not yet command ourselves to discontinue the ongoing mistakes of evolution? One such mistake is our unwillingness to realize the commonality of our goals here on earth as humans, as people. In the great abundance of resources and knowledge we have gathered, why do we still fight over trivial matters? Why do some of us hoard food and goods for ourselves while others starve?

We commit these mistakes and others because we have not yet come to fully believe in ourselves. We have obtained great power, but not yet accepted the responsibility that comes with it. Instead, we outsource the responsibility to good and evil, forces we believe to be innate and uncontrollable. Realistically though, is it not truly the lingering forces of evolution that prompt much that we believe to be evil? Good and evil, right and wrong – these things are recent creations that exist only within the minds of conscious beings. A weed in the earth may grow quickly, using up nutrients and quickly securing a spot in the sun before other plants have a chance to do the same, yet we know this behavior is an evolutionary tactic for survival, not greed. However, in our minds, we associate greed with poor morals, a characteristic we rarely link to evolution.

People struggle, though, to realize the full potential of this new perspective we have been offered by our vast and growing understanding of the world around us. We have taken a step in the right direction, to (generally) label greed as a bad characteristic, but we rarely make a further and necessary connection; greed is an outdated evolutionary tactic for humans.

In many ways, we continue to live like our only purpose is to survive, when really, survival is a given for most people in the developed world. We do not leave our houses in the morning thinking, ‘I hope I survive today.’ It’s a given. It is not, however, foolish or even wrong that evolution has left its mark on us. Evolution is a part of us. We are the products of it.

But, we are now also something more. We are conscious in a new way, a way evolution never was. We can gather large sums of information, consider the past, present and future, and make informed decisions. And, because of this, we know world peace makes sense. We must therefore, actively, push back against evolution where it was wrong. We must say no to absurd behavior based on evolution. We must say yes to our great fortune we’ve been blessed with, our minds, and use it to create a new kind of evolution, one more aware and actively participating in the universe.

June 4, 2018: Europe Trip: Days 1-3

US (Kittery, ME to Boston, MA to Stewart Airport, New York), Norway, Spain and France.

My girlfriend Kelly and I recently returned from a little European adventure. It was supposed to start with a flight out of Boston, MA heading directly to Barcelona, Spain. However... due to a little passport snafu, we ended up spending some 24 hours more in Boston -- much of it in a passport agency, but also some much more enjoyable time unexpectedly staying with our good and welcoming friend Adriana. While not exactly the first day of vacation I imagined, I was able to get a same day passport at the Boston Passport Agency. I've never had much of anything good to say about the process of getting a passport, but the people here were truly incredibly nice, understanding and helped save our trip when we really thought months of planning (and savings) was lost. Some 6 hours after first walking in, I walked out with a new passport.

Of course, at this point we had long missed our original flight, so we scoured the interweb for any way to get to Barcelona that wouldn't entirely break the bank. What we found was a same day $300 some odd dollar flight to Barcelona with a stop over in Bergen, Norway. The catch? It left out of Stewart Airport in New York, a very small airport about 1.5 hours outside NYC, 3 hours drive from Boston and with no public transport to/from Boston where we were to fly back to 11 days later. We were somewhat desperate though and decided we'd figure out the whole car being in a different state thing later. We drove the 3 hours there, left the car in long term parking and just about 24 hours after we were originally supposed to depart, we were on a plain headed to Europe.

We made it to our first stop, Bergen, Norway, with no issues and the view during the decent was enough to put Norway right near the top of my "To travel to" list.

After a layover, we were back on a plane heading towards Barcelona. Our trip was back in sight and it already felt like ages since the Airline attendant said, "You are not going to be able to fly with this passport." Unfortunately, our late arrival to Barcelona meant we didn't have time to see the city. We raced across town to the train station where we immediately hopped on a train. After some train delays, pushing our total travel time up towards the 36 hour mark, we arrived in Avignon, France. After a killer shower and epic sleep, we finally (re)started our adventure. Avignon is a small and beautiful town in the South of France. It is perfectly sized such that it can be crossed by foot in about half an hour. This, however, does not mean there is little to see here. While we only had an evening and morning, I'm sure days could be spent exploring the perfectly French restaurants, cafes, and markets, as well as the stunning Palais des Papes and the famous Pont Saint-Benezet (or simply Pont d'Avignon).

After running around the city, taking in all we could, we took a short train south to the much larger city of Marsellie, a port city of France located on the coast of the Mediterranean Sea. Here we explored the Basilique Notre-Dame de la Garde, a beautiful pebble beach, the old forts of the city, the Iles du Frioul and many cafes with delicious croissants.
